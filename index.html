The Core Challenge

Demand Censoring

In quick commerce, stockout events don't just hurt today's sales — they corrupt tomorrow's forecast. When a product stocks out at 11 AM, the system records only what was sold before the stockout, not what customers actually wanted. This is the fundamental data problem we solve.

📉

Censored Sales ≠ True Demand

A dark store stocking out of fresh strawberries at 11:00 AM may record 100 units sold when genuine customer demand was 200 units. The "missing" 100 units are invisible — not zero demand, but censored demand.

Observed: yt = dt × st When st = 0 (stockout), yt is truncated below true demand dt
🔄

The Self-Reinforcing Cycle

Models trained on censored data underestimate future demand → smaller orders are placed → more stockouts occur → more censored data is generated. This self-reinforcing cycle compounds over time, systematically starving high-demand products of inventory.

The FreshRetailNet-50K dataset shows raw sales carry a −7.37% WPE bias (systematic underestimation) before any recovery is applied.

🏙️

Dark Store Amplification

Unlike traditional retail, dark stores operate with a 3 km service radius, a volatile customer base, and a product mix dominated by short-shelf-life perishables: fresh produce, dairy, seafood. This hyperlocal footprint amplifies both demand volatility and stockout frequency.

30-minute delivery SLA creates acute inventory pressure. Stockout rates peak at 26% by 8 PM.

🚫

Existing Benchmarks Fail

M5, Walmart, and Corporación Favorita — the three dominant public retail forecasting benchmarks — provide no stockout annotations. They treat zero sales as zero demand, making it impossible to train or evaluate censoring-aware models. This gap is precisely what FreshRetailNet-50K fills.

Our problem is MNAR (Missing Not At Random): missingness correlates with demand surges, violating MAR assumptions.

Why MNAR Matters

Standard imputation methods (SAITS, ImputeFormer, CSDI) assume data is Missing At Random (MAR) — that the probability of missingness doesn't depend on the unobserved value. But retail stockouts are fundamentally Missing Not At Random (MNAR): a product stocks out because demand is high. Treating it as MAR systematically underestimates the true demand.

Structured Censoring Mechanisms

Stockouts follow structured patterns. Restocking at 6 AM reduces rates below 2%, but inventory depletes monotonically, reaching 26% stockout rate by 8 PM. Deeper promotions paradoxically increase stockouts (βdiscount = −0.046, p=0.018) by amplifying demand beyond capacity. Rainfall shifts demand from outdoor to online markets (βrain = +0.108). These structured dependencies are the hallmark of true MNAR data.

What makes FRN-50K unique?

The dataset spans 3 months of real operational data (March–June 2024) from Dingdong Inc., China's leading quick-commerce operator. Every stockout event is verified and annotated at hourly resolution — not inferred from inventory gaps.

The hours_stock_status column contains a 24-element binary array where 1 = stockout, 0 = in-stock. The derived stock_hour6_22_cnt counts stockout hours in the 6 AM–10 PM operating window. A day is classified as in-stock when this count ≤ 2.

This annotation granularity enables us to build the ground truth needed for MNAR simulation: we mask known in-stock days at a 15% rate (proportional to demand) to create synthetic censored regions with verifiable ground truth.

Key Statistical Properties

Temporal Cyclicity
Weekdays show bimodal peaks at 09:00 (pre-lunch) and 16:00 (pre-dinner). Weekends feature a single amplified morning surge at 09:00 with ~2× weekday peak intensity, driven by household bulk purchasing.
Power-Law Demand
Demand follows a power-law distribution with α = 2.83 (KS p < 0.03). The top 20% of SKUs account for 51.8% of all transactions — creating a long-tail forecasting challenge where sparse SKUs dominate the catalog.
MNAR Censoring
Stockout rates follow a U-shaped diurnal cycle: below 2% post-restock at 6 AM, rising monotonically to 26% by 8 PM. Promotions increase stockout probability (β = −0.046, p = 0.018); rainfall increases it further (β = +0.108, p = 0.05).
Promotional Effects
Active promotions yield a median sales uplift of 1.43× (IQR: 1.05×–2.06×). Holiday periods carry a 1.27× demand multiplier. Deeper discounts paradoxically increase stockouts by amplifying demand beyond inventory capacity.
Weather Effects
Rainfall increases vegetable demand by 11% via offline-to-online market substitution. Temperature has category-divergent effects: rising temperatures increase frozen goods stockouts (β = +0.065) but decrease meat shortages (β = −0.067).
Hierarchy Structure
Four-level product hierarchy (city → store → category 1 → category 2 → SKU) and three-level category taxonomy (863 SKUs across 5 major perishable categories). City-store geographic hierarchy enables regional analysis.

System Design

Two-Stage Pipeline

Our pipeline decouples demand recovery from demand forecasting. Stage 1 imputes what was truly demanded during stockouts. Stage 2 uses that clean signal to build a 7-day forecast. Presented by Ayush Vaibhav Gond.

Stage 1

Latent Demand Recovery

Corrects historical data · Removes MNAR bias

01

Raw Data Ingestion

Load FreshRetailNet-50K Parquet files. Decode hourly arrays. Flip stock_status encoding (1 = stockout). Derive in_stock flag (cnt ≤ 2).

02

Feature Engineering (62 features)

Build 5 feature groups: Calendar (10), Promotional (5), Weather (10), Lag/Rolling (16), Entity/Hourly (21). Lag features use sale_for_lag — NaN on stockout days to prevent censored zero contamination.

causality rule applied
03

MNAR Simulation

Synthetically mask 15% of in-stock rows (demand-proportional probability) to create censored validation regions with known ground truth. Last 20% by date → validation set.

split date: 2024-06-08
04

LightGBM with Tweedie Objective

Train on 2.7M in-stock rows with power-law sample weights (wᵢ ∝ μᵢ^(1/α)). Tweedie log-link handles zero-inflated power-law demand (α = 2.83). Custom WAPE feval for early stopping.

p=1.5 · 345 trees · ES@150
05

Post-hoc Bias Calibration

Compute scalar c = Σy_t / Σd̂_t on in-stock validation rows. Multiply all predictions by c. Clamp to [0.80, 1.25] for safety.

c = 1.0748 (LightGBM)
06

Demand Reconstruction

Apply recovery formula: d_t = y_t · s_t + d̂_t · (1 − s_t). In-stock days retain observed sales; stockout days receive model-imputed demand with mean 1.33× uplift.

06

7-Day Forecast Output

Final calibrated ensemble forecasts ens_h1 through ens_h7 power all dashboard modules: demand overview, stockout countdown, smart replenishment list.

Eq. 1 — Censoring Mechanism
sₜ = 1 if iₜ > 0 (in-stock) sₜ = 0 if iₜ = 0 (stockout)

Binary stock status indicator derived from hourly stockout count. A day is in-stock if stock_hour6_22_cnt ≤ 2 (at most 2 stockout hours in operating window).

Eq. 2 — Demand Recovery
dₜ = yₜ ⊙ sₜ + d̂ₜ ⊙ (1 − sₜ)

Element-wise combination. Observed sales on in-stock days; model-imputed demand on stockout days. The imputed d̂ₜ averages 1.33× the censored floor.

Eq. 3 — Bias Calibration
c = Σ yₜ / Σ d̂ₜ (over in-stock rows) c ∈ [0.80, 1.25]

Post-hoc scalar calibration analogous to Platt scaling. Stage 2 uses per-horizon cₕ to correct horizon-varying bias.

Eq. 4 — WAPE Metric
WAPE = Σ|dₜ − yₜ| / Σ yₜ

Weighted Absolute Percentage Error. Demand-weighted, so high-volume SKUs dominate. More stable than MAPE on zero-inflated data. Primary Stage 1 early-stopping metric.

Eq. 5 — WPE (Bias)
WPE = Σ(dₜ − yₜ) / Σ yₜ Positive = overestimate Negative = underestimate

Weighted Percentage Error. Tracks systematic bias direction. Target: |WPE| < 3%. All Stage 2 models show negative WPE without calibration.

Eq. 6 — Decoupling Score ρ_DS
ρ_DS = Σᵢ wᵢ · Pearson(SRᵢ, dᵢ) wᵢ = μᵢ / Σⱼ μⱼ

Demand-Supply Decoupling Score. Weighted Pearson across all store-product pairs between stockout ratio and recovered demand mean. Target: near zero. Our result: +0.46.

Causality Rule (Critical Design Decision)

Supply-side features from hours_stock_status are safe for the current day — supply status is observable before demand is realized. Demand-side features from hours_sale must be shifted by one day to prevent data leakage. Using today's sales pattern as a predictor for today's demand constitutes causality inversion.

Why the Tweedie Objective?

The Tweedie distribution is an exponential family distribution particularly suited to zero-inflated, right-skewed, power-law data. The variance function is Var(Y) ∝ E[Y]^p, where p is the variance power. For p=1 (Poisson), variance equals mean. For p=2 (Gamma), variance is proportional to mean squared. We use p=1.5, placing us between these — matching the compound Poisson-Gamma structure of retail demand.

The log-link function means the model predicts in log-space, ensuring non-negative outputs without explicit constraints. This is critical for demand data where the distribution spans 3 orders of magnitude and negative predictions are inadmissible.

Random Forest, lacking this objective, must rely entirely on sample reweighting — which partially compensates but cannot replicate log-space optimization. This explains the 0.67 pp WAPE gap between RF (28.50%) and LightGBM (27.83%).

Tweedie Log-Likelihood

L = −y · exp(−ρ(1−p)·θ) / (1−p)
   + exp(ρ(2−p)·θ) / (2−p)

where θ = log(μ) [log-link]
p = 1.5 [variance power]

Variance: Var(Y) ∝ μ^1.5
Power-Law Sample Weights:

wᵢ ∝ μᵢ^(1/α)  [α = 2.83]
Clamped to [0.1, 10]
Normalized to unit mean

Up-weights high-demand pairs

Stage 2 — Deep Dive

7-Day Demand Forecasting

Three forecasting paradigms — statistical baseline, gradient boosting, and neural network — trained on all 50,000 store-product pairs using the clean Stage 1 recovered demand signal. Presented by Parth Sidhu & Srijan Gupta.

SSA

Statistical Baseline

Similar Scenario Average — a three-level fallback lookup over historical in-stock days. Forecasts demand at horizon h by averaging historical recovered demand conditioned on matching (store, SKU, day-of-week) triplets. Falls back to (SKU, DOW) if the triplet has too few observations, then to (store, SKU) average as a final fallback.

SSA's 39.94% WAPE and −11.78% WPE establish the baseline difficulty of the forecasting task. The significant negative WPE indicates systematic underestimation even on a historical average — high-demand days are more likely to stockout and thus excluded from the average.

LightGBM

Multi-Horizon Gradient Boosting

Seven independent LightGBM models, one per forecast horizon (h=1 through h=7). A direct multi-step strategy that stacks all seven horizons with the horizon index h as a feature. This avoids the recursive error accumulation problem of autoregressive forecasting.

The dominant Stage 2 feature by gain is Column_37 (recovered demand lag from Stage 1) — confirming that the Stage 1 clean signal provides the primary predictive information and justifying the two-stage design.

MLP

Tabular Neural Network

A shallow fully-connected network taking the same 64-dimensional tabular feature vector as LightGBM. Architecture: BatchNorm → Linear(64→256) → ReLU → Dropout(0.2) → Linear(256→128) → ReLU → Dropout(0.2) → Linear(128→1) → Softplus.

The Softplus activation at the output ensures strictly positive predictions while providing smooth, continuous gradients — unlike ReLU which has a hard zero cutoff. This yields the lowest systematic bias among all Stage 2 models (−5.86% WPE) without explicit calibration.

Ensemble

Weighted LGB + MLP

Per-horizon weighted combination of LightGBM and MLP predictions. The optimal weight w*ₕ is determined per forecast day by grid search on in-stock test rows minimizing WAPE. LightGBM is dominant throughout (w* ∈ [0.75, 1.00]), but MLP contributes meaningful correction at near horizons (h=1–3).

What We Learned

Key Insights

Six distilled findings from building, running, and analyzing the two-stage pipeline across 50,000 store-product pairs.

01

Tweedie Objective is Non-Negotiable for Stage 1

The log-link Tweedie loss (p=1.5) inherently models power-law zero-inflated demand. LightGBM and XGBoost both use it and outperform Random Forest which relies on sample weighting alone — a 0.67 pp WAPE gap (RF 28.50% vs LGB 27.83%). For demand data with α = 2.83, the loss function choice matters more than model architecture.

0.67ppWAPE gap RF→LGB
02

Data Coverage > Model Complexity

LightGBM trained on all 50,000 store-product pairs (3.8M rows) achieves 29.36% Stage 2 WAPE — matching the paper's Temporal Fusion Transformer without attention mechanisms, sequence padding, or GPU compute. The ensemble improves only 0.06 pp over standalone LightGBM, confirming diminishing returns once training data is complete.

0.06ppEnsemble gain over LGB
03

Post-Hoc Calibration is the Primary WPE Control

A single scalar c = 1.0748 reduces Stage 1 WPE from −7.37% (raw sales baseline) to +0.00%. Per-horizon calibration factors c_h ∈ [1.052, 1.109] in Stage 2 account for increasing underestimation as lag features become stale. Calibration is more powerful than architecture changes for bias control.

−7.37%→0%WPE via calibration
04

MLP Softplus Yields Lowest Systematic Bias

Despite higher WAPE (30.69%), the MLP achieves the lowest WPE of all Stage 2 models (−5.86%) without explicit calibration. The Softplus output activation and Huber loss training regime intrinsically regularize scale errors, avoiding the hard truncation of ReLU that can amplify bias in gradient boosting tree splits.

05

Our Stage 1 ρ_DS of +0.46 vs TimesNet's +0.07 is the clearest signal of what's left to improve. High-demand pairs have higher stockout rates, concentrating supply-demand correlation in top SKUs. This structural confound cannot be fully disentangled by a single-stage tabular model.

06

NaN Lag Masking Preserves 68.7% of Rows

A key design decision: lag features use sale_for_lag (NaN on stockout days) rather than the raw censored sale_amount (0 on stockout days). Censored zeros contaminate rolling statistics and lags, causing the model to learn that demand is low when it's actually censored. NaN propagation preserves 68.7% of rows that would otherwise be dropped.

Summary

Conclusion

We built a two-stage pipeline for censored demand forecasting on FreshRetailNet-50K, demonstrating that lightweight gradient boosting with the right loss function can match computationally expensive deep learning — while remaining interpretable and deployable.

01

Tweedie is Essential

Both LightGBM and XGBoost outperform Random Forest. The log-link loss function, not tree count or depth, is the key differentiator for power-law demand recovery.

02

MLP Provides Bias Complement

The Softplus activation and Huber loss give MLP (−5.86% WPE) lower bias than LightGBM (−7.81%). Ensemble blending captures both WAPE accuracy and bias control (29.30% final WAPE).

03

ρ_DS = +0.46 Guides Future Work

Residual supply-demand correlation is the principal remaining gap. Future work: demand-supply decoupling, quantile-aware Stage 2 training, real-time dashboard ingestion.
